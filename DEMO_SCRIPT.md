## Opening (30 seconds)

**Say this:**

> "This is AIPM — Autonomous AI Product Manager. It's a multi-agent LLM pipeline that takes a product idea or input bundle — tickets, customer notes, competitor briefs, metrics — and runs it through 8 specialized AI agents to produce real PM artifacts: a PRD, a roadmap, an experiment plan, a decision log, a backlog, and a final plan with a go/no-go recommendation."
>
> "I'm going to show you five things: first, how it handles a high-risk idea; second, how it responds to a metric drop; third, how it builds a roadmap for competitive pressure; fourth, how one YAML policy change flips the recommendation; and fifth, the full set of artifacts it produces."

**Duration:** ~3-4 minutes

### What to type (pick one)

**Say this:**

It also has customer notes saying 40% of users have privacy concerns, a competitor brief showing Competitor A was fined for GDPR violations, and a legal memo flagging PII issues."

## SCENARIO 2 — Metric Drop (SearchBoost Query Engine)

**Duration:** ~3 minutes

### What to type

```powershell
$base = "output/demo_02_metric_drop/dd2d9204-3a5d-4b68-b763-744acc2abb70"
```

---

### STEP 1 — Introduce the scenario

**Say this:**

> "This bundle is SearchBoost Query Engine. The problem: search relevance scores dropped 15% after deploying version 2.4.1. The bundle has 7 tickets including the relevance drop, user complaints about irrelevant results, increased mobile latency, missing analytics events for new query types, and an inconclusive A/B test. Metrics show relevance went from 0.85 to 0.72, and the zero result rate is 18%."
>
> "What we want to see is: does the system produce a proper experiment plan and flag the instrumentation gaps?"

---

### STEP 2 — Show the experiment plan

**Type:**
```powershell
code "$base/artifacts/experiment_plan.md"
```

**Say this:**

> "Here's the experiment plan. The hypothesis is clear: 'If we improve the SearchBoost algorithm to enhance result relevance, then the Search Relevance Score will increase by 5.88 percent — from 0.85 to 0.9 — because users will find results more aligned with their queries.' It cites metrics-001 as the evidence."
>
> "Success metrics are defined in a table: Search Relevance Score baseline 0.85, target 0.9, measured by 7-day trailing average over 30 days. Search CTR baseline 0.47, target 0.5."
>
> "There's a guardrail metric: Session Abandonment After Search — currently at 0.28, max acceptable is 0.25. If it degrades past that, we stop."
>
> "The experiment design is a 4-phase rollout: 10% for 7 days, 25% for 7 days, 50% for 7 days, then 100%. At each phase, evaluate success and guardrails. If relevance doesn't hit at least 0.87 after phase one, or if abandonment exceeds 0.28, halt and reassess."
>
> "Sample size is 1000 users per phase — that comes directly from the policy file's min_sample_size setting."
>
> "Rollback criteria are explicit. Analysis plan uses paired t-test for the scores and chi-square for abandonment proportions, with Holm-Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons."

---

### STEP 3 — Show instrumentation gaps

**Type:**
```powershell
(Get-Content "$base/findings/metrics.json" -Raw | ConvertFrom-Json).findings | Select-Object id, title, type | Format-Table
```

**Say this:**

> "Now look at what the Metrics Agent found. Five findings total:
>
> - metrics-001: North Star Metric defined as Search Relevance Score — type metric, critical impact.
> - metrics-002: Primary KPI is Search Click-Through Rate.
> - metrics-003: Guardrail Metric is Session Abandonment After Search.
> - metrics-004: This is the key one — Instrumentation Gap for New Query Types — type gap, critical impact. The system detected that events like image_search_query, voice_search_query, and filter_applied are missing.
> - metrics-005: Recommendation to establish baseline metrics for the recently launched image search.
>
> So it didn't just build an experiment plan — it told you what you're missing to even measure properly."

---

**Transition:**

> "Metric drop handled — experiment plan with hypothesis, guardrails, rollback criteria, and the system flagged instrumentation gaps. Next: competitive pressure."

## SCENARIO 4 — Policy Configurability (Same Input, Different Outcome)

**Duration:** ~3-4 minutes

---

### STEP 1 — Show the two policy files

**Say this:**

> "I'm going to show you the same privacy_risk bundle — the PersonaLens User Profiling we saw in Scenario 1 — run through two different policies. The default policy and the startup fast policy."

**Type:**
```powershell
Write-Host "=== DEFAULT POLICY (key settings) ==="; Select-String "max_unmitigated|require_legal_review" src/aipm/policies/default_policy.yaml; Write-Host ""; Write-Host "=== STARTUP POLICY (key settings) ==="; Select-String "max_unmitigated|require_legal_review" src/aipm/policies/startup_fast_policy.yaml
```

**Say this:**

> "Two key differences in the YAML:
>
> Default policy: max_unmitigated_high_risks is 2, and require_legal_review_for_pii is true.
>
> Startup policy: max_unmitigated_high_risks is 5, and require_legal_review_for_pii is false.
>
> That's it. Two numbers changed. Let's see what happens."

---

### STEP 2 — Compare recommendations

**Type:**
```powershell
$a = (Get-Content "output/demo_11a_default_policy/8a1afe3b-82b0-44e4-b2b8-76abd4a9f3f9/artifacts/final_plan.json" -Raw | ConvertFrom-Json).recommendation; $b = (Get-Content "output/demo_11b_startup_policy/a8348979-0ada-4952-a88f-9145d66087c4/artifacts/final_plan.json" -Raw | ConvertFrom-Json).recommendation; Write-Host "DEFAULT POLICY  -> $($a.decision)"; Write-Host "STARTUP POLICY  -> $($b.decision)"
```

**Say this:**

> "Default policy: do not pursue. Startup policy: validate first."
>
> "Same product idea. Same tickets. Same customer notes. Same competitor data. The only difference is the policy YAML. The default policy killed it. The startup policy said: there are real risks, but you can validate and proceed."

---

### STEP 3 — Explain WHY the outcome changed

**Type:**
```powershell
Write-Host "=== DEFAULT RISK GATE ==="; Get-Content "output/demo_11a_default_policy/8a1afe3b-82b0-44e4-b2b8-76abd4a9f3f9/findings/risk_gate_result.json"; Write-Host ""; Write-Host "=== STARTUP RISK GATE ==="; Get-Content "output/demo_11b_startup_policy/a8348979-0ada-4952-a88f-9145d66087c4/findings/risk_gate_result.json"
```

**Say this:**

> "Look at the risk gates side by side."
>
> "Default policy has 3 blockers:
> 1. Inadequate Consent Mechanism — risk-001, critical.
> 2. GDPR Compliance Audit Required — risk-002, critical.
> 3. And this one: '3 unmitigated high risks exceed maximum of 2.' That's the policy rule. The default allows 2 max. We had 3. Blocked."
>
> "Plus 2 warnings about legal review required for PII — because require_legal_review_for_pii is true."
>
> "Now the startup policy: only 2 blockers — the same consent and GDPR ones, because those are critical and block_on_critical_privacy is still true even in startup mode. But the third blocker disappeared — because max_unmitigated_high_risks is 5, and we only have 3. Under the limit now."
>
> "And zero warnings — because require_legal_review_for_pii is false in startup mode."
>
> "So the startup policy is saying: critical privacy risks still block you — we're not reckless — but we're more tolerant of high-severity risks and we don't require legal review at this stage. That's enough to move the recommendation from 'kill it' to 'validate and proceed.'"
>
> "This is the power of the policy engine. Product teams can tune risk tolerance without changing any code."

> "Finally, let me show you the full artifact set."
